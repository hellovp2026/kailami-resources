Video Editor mock task

Build your editing portfolio.

You don't need to record yourself. You don't need a studio. You just need good footage and your editing skills. Here's how to build 3 portfolio pieces without ever being on camera.

Tools needed
CapCut (free) or DaVinci Resolve
Time
8–12 hours total
On camera?
Nope, never
00
Where to get footage (read this first)

Before any editing, you need footage. You will NOT be recording yourself. Here are 3 legit ways to get editable clips.

Option 1 — Royalty-free stock (easiest)

Free stock video sites let you download clips anyone can use. Quality is surprisingly good. This is the fastest way.

  • → Pexels Videos — biggest free library
  • → Pixabay Videos — also free
  • → Mixkit — clean, modern clips
Option 2 — Edit for a friend or family member

Ask someone you know who talks on camera (a friend with a small YouTube channel, a family member who records events, anyone who has raw footage sitting around). Offer to edit ONE video for them for free in exchange for using it in your portfolio. You get real client-style footage. They get a free edit. Everyone wins.

Option 3 — Volunteer edit for a small creator

Find a small YouTuber or TikToker (under 5k followers) whose content you like. DM them: "I'm a beginner editor building a portfolio. Can I edit one of your videos for free? You own the final edit, I just want to use it in my portfolio." Most say yes — small creators always need help. You get real content + a testimonial if they like the work.

Rule
Never use content you didn't get permission to use. Don't rip someone's YouTube video and re-edit it for your portfolio unless they said yes.
01
Short-form reel (IG / TikTok / Shorts)

Reels are the #1 thing clients pay editors for. Build one and you're instantly more hireable.

1
Grab 2–3 short clips on one theme

From Pexels or Pixabay. Pick a theme: productivity, morning routine, travel, fitness, food. 5–10 seconds per clip is plenty.

2
Write a simple script or hook

Don't record it. Just write text overlays. Examples:

  • "3 morning habits that changed my life"
  • "POV: you finally started freelancing"
  • "The one app I wish I found sooner"

Your text overlays will tell the story — no voiceover needed.

3
Edit for retention
  • Cut every 1–2 seconds — no slow shots
  • Add big text overlays that reinforce your story
  • Grab a trending sound from IG or TikTok's library
  • Zoom in on key moments
  • End with a CTA text ("save this" or "follow for more")
Why this matters
Clients measure editors by retention. Fast cuts + big text + trending audio = more views = you keep getting hired.
4
Export vertical at 1080x1920

H.264 format, 30fps. Upload it to YouTube as unlisted so you can drop the link in your portfolio.

What goes in your portfolio
  • Link to the finished reel (unlisted YouTube)
  • Screenshot of your editing timeline showing cuts + text
  • The concept / hook you wrote before editing
02
Long-form YouTube edit

Long-form editors charge more and keep retainer clients. Proving you can edit a 3–5 min video well = higher pay, more stability.

1
Get footage from one of the 3 sources above

Best options for this one:

  • Edit a free vlog-style clip from Pexels
  • Edit a friend or family member's existing raw footage
  • Volunteer-edit one video for a small YouTuber

You want 5–10 minutes of raw footage to cut down to 3–5 min.

2
Edit it to a polished 3–5 min final

Your edit needs:

  • An intro title card or animated logo (2–4 seconds)
  • A strong hook in the first 15 seconds — cut any slow intro
  • Cut all filler words (um, uh, like, you know) — this alone transforms a video
  • B-roll over key moments (use Pexels for this too)
  • Lower-third text for important points
  • Background music from YouTube Audio Library (free)
  • An outro with CTA text (subscribe, watch next)
3
Color correct the footage

Even basic color correction makes amateur footage look professional. Adjust exposure, white balance, and apply a subtle preset across all clips so everything looks consistent.

4
Clean the audio
  • Normalize volume — all clips at similar levels
  • Remove background hiss / room noise
  • Music should soften when someone speaks (it's called "ducking")

Clients care about audio. Bad audio = they won't rehire you.

5
Export and upload

1920x1080, H.264, 30fps. Upload to YouTube as unlisted. Grab the link for your portfolio.

What goes in your portfolio
  • Link to the edited YouTube video (unlisted)
  • Before/after — a snippet of raw vs. edited
  • Timeline screenshot showing your work
03
Podcast clip edit

Every coach, founder, and creator is starting a podcast. They all need someone to pull viral clips. This skill alone can get you hired.

1
Find free podcast footage

Options:

  • Pexels has free interview-style stock clips
  • Ask a small podcaster if you can edit a clip for their show (tag them when you post it)
  • If you know someone with a podcast — offer to clip one episode for them free

Do not rip someone's podcast and republish it without permission.

2
Find the best 30–60 seconds

Listen to the full episode (or use a transcript). Pull the most punchy moment — a hot take, a story, a surprising insight. It should stand alone and hit emotionally.

3
Make 2 versions: vertical + square

Clients want clips for multiple platforms:

  • Vertical (9:16): for Reels, TikTok, Shorts
  • Square (1:1): for LinkedIn, Facebook, Twitter

Reposition the speaker's face so they stay centered in both ratios.

4
Add captions + polish
  • Auto-generate captions in CapCut, then clean them up manually
  • Make captions big, bold, and on-brand (match the podcast's colors)
  • Add a title card: "What [guest] said about X"
  • Small zoom-ins during key moments
  • Quiet background music if it fits
5
Export both versions

Upload both to YouTube unlisted or a portfolio folder. Show clients you can deliver platform-specific content, not just one format.

What goes in your portfolio
  • Both versions (vertical + square)
  • Before/after — raw clip vs. your polished version
  • Timeline screenshot showing captions + reframe work
